A/

Search on the side · live trip timeline

A persistent left input column collects route → flight → pax progressively. The right canvas is the trip story — empty at first, then a growing timeline once an airport is detected. Timing is the anxiety in an airport transfer, not route geometry.

recommend
Visibility of system status Progressive disclosure Recognition over recall The left panel never disappears, so a user can always see where they are in the input set. The right side does the heavy lifting of explaining what we'll do for them — when the driver arrives, how long we wait, when they reach the gate. URLs change on real step changes (landing → flight → cars); flight selection is in-page state inside /rides/flight.

B/

Expanding search widget

A compact top-of-page widget — same visual language as the Flights/Hotels search bar — that grows as inputs are added. Airport detected → date field injects → flight-search field injects → selected flight locks as a confirmed row inside the widget. Cars render below.

consider
Jakob's Law Aesthetic-usability Progressive disclosure The strongest argument: it looks and behaves like the rest of Almosafer. Risk: airport-transfer timing is the user's actual anxiety, and a horizontal widget has limited room to tell that story above the fold — the timeline / system-status moments are pushed below the car cards or into a smaller side panel.

C/

Adaptive shell · widget at rest, side panel at depth

Honest about the fact that an airport transfer is genuinely a different beast. Starts as B's compact widget (familiar, low-effort). The moment an airport is detected, the layout morphs into A's side-panel + timeline for the flight step — where complexity actually lives. On the cars page, returns to a summary strip. URLs follow the morph.

strong hybrid
Aesthetic-usability Visibility of system status Match the mental model Familiar shell where the task is simple (N→N feels like Flights/Hotels). Specialist shell where the task is hard (A→N needs the timeline). One product, two postures. Risk to manage: the morph itself must feel intentional — not jarring — and the user must understand they're still on the same flow.

How to choose between A, B, C

  • A · Side panel. Pick if airport transfers are the majority of bookings or if reducing arrival anxiety is the #1 north-star. The timeline is its single biggest asset.
  • B · Expanding widget. Pick if N→N rides outnumber A→N, and Almosafer's other widgets are well-loved. Lowest learning cost; pays a price on the flight step's clarity.
  • C · Adaptive shell. Pick if the team has the engineering appetite for the morph transition. Probably the strongest end-to-end UX, but the most to build and animate well.

Cross-cutting decisions still open

  • Default sort on /cars — recommended vs price?
  • How loudly do we surface delay-tracking promise on landing?
  • Should "Hourly" live as a tile here or as a sibling product page?
  • Tablet — does C's morph degrade gracefully or revert to B?
